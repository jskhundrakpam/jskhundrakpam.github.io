Before getting into this, let's quickly go over how light bends when it moves between different materials. That's refraction.

When light passes from one medium to another at an angle, it bends. The medium where light travels slower is called **optically denser** (like glass or water), and where it travels faster is **optically rarer** (like air).

Now here's what happens when light goes from denser to rarer - say from water to air. It speeds up and bends away from the normal (that imaginary line perpendicular to the surface). Increase the angle of incidence, and the refraction angle increases too. At some point, you hit a specific angle where the refracted ray runs exactly along the boundary - the angle of refraction becomes 90°. That's the **critical angle**.

Go past that critical angle, and something interesting happens. The light doesn't exit the denser medium anymore. Instead, it reflects back inside, following the usual laws of reflection.

So why call it "Total Internal Reflection"?

Well, even during normal refraction, a small amount of light gets reflected back internally. But when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, there's no refraction at all. Every bit of that light gets reflected back inside. All of it. That's why it's "total" - complete reflection happening within the same medium.

### Where this actually shows up

This isn't just a textbook thing - it's behind quite a few everyday technologies.

Take fiber optics. Those thin glass cables that carry internet data across oceans? They work because light keeps bouncing off the inner walls at angles greater than the critical angle, trapped inside the fiber for miles with almost no signal loss. Same principle behind endoscopes that let doctors look inside your body without cutting it open.

You've probably used prismatic binoculars without realizing it - they use prisms that bounce light internally to make the binoculars shorter and easier to hold. Even some fingerprint scanners use a variation of this (frustrated total internal reflection) to capture the ridges of your finger.

So next time you stream something or use binoculars, there's a bit of this physics quietly doing its thing.
